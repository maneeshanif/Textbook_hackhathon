# 3.3 Actuator Technologies

## Electric Motors

### DC Brushless Motors (BLDC)
**Principle**: Permanent magnets on rotor, electronically commutated stator.

**Advantages**:
- High efficiency (85-95%)
- Long lifespan (no brush wear)
- Precise control

**Applications**: Most modern humanoid robots (Tesla Optimus, Unitree H1)

### Motor Specifications

**Torque Constant** ($K_t$):

$$
\tau = K_t \cdot I
$$

**Speed Constant** ($K_v$):

$$
\omega_{\text{no-load}} = K_v \cdot V
$$

**Gear Ratio**: Increases torque but reduces speed

$$
\tau_{\text{output}} = G \cdot \tau_{\text{motor}}, \quad \omega_{\text{output}} = \frac{\omega_{\text{motor}}}{G}
$$

Where $G$ is the gear ratio.

### Harmonic Drive
A precision gear system used in humanoid robots.

**Advantages**:
- High torque in compact package
- Zero backlash (important for control)

**Gear Ratios**: 30:1 to 160:1

## Hydraulic Actuators

### Principle
Use pressurized fluid to generate force.

**Advantages**:
- **Very high power-to-weight ratio**
- Fast response time

**Disadvantages**:
- Complex hydraulic systems (pumps, valves, hoses)
- Maintenance-intensive
- Noisy

### Example: Boston Dynamics Atlas
- Uses hydraulic actuators for high dynamic performance
- Can perform backflips and parkour
- Requires large onboard hydraulic pump

## Series Elastic Actuators (SEA)

### Principle
Add a **compliant spring** between motor and load.

```
Motor → Gearbox → Spring → Output Link
```

**Advantages**:
1. **Shock absorption**: Protects motor from impacts
2. **Force control**: Measure spring deflection to estimate torque
3. **Energy storage**: Spring stores energy during motion

**Disadvantages**:
- Reduced bandwidth (slower response)
- More complex mechanical design

### Torque Estimation

$$
\tau = K_s (\theta_{\text{motor}} - \theta_{\text{output}})
$$

Where $K_s$ is the spring stiffness.

### Applications
- NASA Valkyrie humanoid
- Rethink Robotics Baxter (collaborative robot)

## Actuator Comparison

| Actuator Type | Power-to-Weight | Efficiency | Compliance | Cost | Maintenance |
|---------------|-----------------|------------|------------|------|-------------|
| **BLDC Electric** | Medium | High (85-95%) | Low | Low | Low |
| **Hydraulic** | Very High | Medium (60-80%) | Low | High | High |
| **SEA** | Medium | Medium | High | Medium | Medium |
| **Pneumatic** | Low | Low (20-40%) | High | Low | Medium |

## Motor Selection Example

**Requirements**:
- Joint torque: 100 N·m
- Joint speed: 3 rad/s
- Duty cycle: Continuous

**Motor Specs**:
- Motor torque: 0.5 N·m
- Motor speed: 3000 RPM = 314 rad/s
- Motor power: 157 W

**Required Gear Ratio**:

$$
G = \frac{\tau_{\text{required}}}{\tau_{\text{motor}}} = \frac{100}{0.5} = 200
$$

**Output Speed**:

$$
\omega_{\text{output}} = \frac{314}{200} = 1.57 \text{ rad/s}
$$

**Problem**: Output speed is too low (need 3 rad/s). Must select a more powerful motor or accept lower torque.

## Key Takeaways

- **BLDC Motors**: Most common in modern humanoids, efficient and precise
- **Hydraulic**: High power-to-weight but complex systems
- **Series Elastic Actuators**: Compliant, good for interaction but slower
- **Gear Ratio**: Trades off torque and speed
